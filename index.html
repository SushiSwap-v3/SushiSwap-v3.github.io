What is SushiSwap v3?

SushiSwap v3 is a non-custodial automated market maker: pools of two tokens, priced by a curve inside smart contracts, with no order book and no intermediary holding your funds. It follows the concentrated-liquidity architecture popularized by Uniswap v3 but runs on Sushi's own deployments, interface and routing. Instead of spreading capital across every price from zero to infinity as in SushiSwap v2, a liquidity provider picks a specific price range and a fee tier, and the position earns pool fees only while trades execute inside that range. That makes capital work harder per dollar deposited, but it also means positions go inactive when price leaves the range and need active management. Each position is represented by an ERC-721-compatible NFT carrying the pool, range, liquidity and fee-accounting state. The core logic lives in the open-source v3-core contracts, with routing and position management in the periphery layer.

How it works

A swap is one atomic on-chain transaction. You pick a network, input token, output token, amount and slippage tolerance; the router quotes a path through one or more pools; your wallet signs; the pool contracts compute output from available liquidity, deduct the pool fee, enforce your minimum received, and settle everything in a single transaction or revert it entirely. Three things deserve attention before you sign. First, price impact: the bigger your trade relative to liquidity along the route, the worse your execution price, and the interface's impact figure is the number to read, not the spot price. Second, minimum received: your slippage setting converts to a hard floor the contract enforces, so a move beyond it reverts the swap rather than filling you badly. Third, approvals: spending an ERC-20 for the first time requires a separate approval transaction granting the router permission to move that token — that is a standing allowance, not the swap itself, and it costs its own gas.

Networks and tokens

Ethereum mainnet is chain ID 1, and its verified SushiSwap v3 factory is 0xbACEB8eC6b9355Dfc0269C18bac9d6E2Bdc29C4F. Sushi also publishes deployments across numerous other EVM networks, and the practical rule is that each chain has its own pools, its own liquidity depth and its own factory — a pair that trades well on one network may be thin or absent on another. Pools are identified by chain, token pair and fee tier, so the same two tokens can exist as several distinct pools. Pool assets use the ERC-20 interface; native coins like ETH are handled through their wrapped versions inside the contracts. Before assuming a route exists, check the live app on the specific network you intend to use, and cross-check aggregate activity on an independent tracker such as the SushiSwap v3 protocol dashboard. You do not need the SUSHI token to swap or to open a position.

Fees and costs

Every swap pays three separate things, and conflating them is how people misjudge a trade. One, the pool fee: each pool belongs to a fee tier, the fee is deducted from the swap output by the contract itself, and it goes to the liquidity providers active in the traded range — multiple tiers can exist for the same pair, so the route chosen affects what you pay. Two, network gas: paid to the chain's validators in the native token, varying with congestion and with how many pools the route touches, and it applies to approvals, swaps, and position management alike. Three, price impact: not a fee but a real cost, the slippage between the quoted spot price and your execution price caused by your own size against available liquidity. On the LP side, positions earn a pro-rata share of pool fees only while in range, and ranges, tiers and impermanent loss are covered in Sushi's notes on what v3 LPs need to know. No SUSHI is required for ordinary use.

Is SushiSwap v3 safe?

SushiSwap v3 removes centralized custody — you keep your keys and sign everything yourself — but that is not the same as risk-free. The contracts are open-source, which lets anyone inspect them, yet open-source code can still contain bugs, and the honest residual risks are: smart-contract exploits in pool or routing contracts, malicious or worthless tokens that mimic legitimate tickers, phishing sites and fake interfaces that harvest signatures, adverse price movement and impermanent loss for LPs, and standing token approvals that outlive the swap you made them for. Approval risk deserves special attention: an approval grants a spender an allowance over your token, so verify the spender address and the amount, and consider revoking stale allowances. Cross-chain activity adds bridge risk on top. For a grounded overview of how these pieces fit together, ethereum.org's introduction to DeFi is a reasonable neutral starting point. None of this is a reason to avoid the protocol; it is a reason to verify before you sign.

How to swap

Connect your wallet to the Sushi app and confirm you are on the network where your tokens actually sit. Select the input token and output token — paste the token's contract address rather than trusting a ticker search if the asset is obscure — and enter the amount. Read the quoted route: which pools it touches, the fee tier, the price impact, and the minimum received at your slippage setting. If this is the first time spending that token, sign the approval transaction first and check the spender and allowance amount in your wallet prompt. Then sign the swap itself and wait for the network's confirmation; settlement follows that chain's block inclusion and finality rules, so timing varies by network. If the quoted route looks wrong — odd pools, high impact, unexpected tokens in the path — cancel and re-quote rather than signing anyway.

Problems and fixes

Wrong network: the most common failure. If the app shows no balance or no route, check which chain your wallet is connected to before anything else. No gas: every action, including approvals and failed transactions, needs the network's native token, so keep a small balance on each chain you use. Approval stuck or repeated: an approval only covers one token for one spender; a new token or a revoked allowance requires signing it again, and a pending approval can block the swap behind it. High price impact warning: your size is large relative to liquidity on the route — split the trade, try a different fee-tier pool, or accept that the pair is thin on this network. Transaction reverted: usually slippage exceeded between quote and execution, or price moved; raise the slippage tolerance slightly and re-quote, but never loosen it blindly to force a fill. Tokens not showing after the swap: add the token's contract address to your wallet manually; the balance may be there with the asset simply unlisted in the wallet UI.

SushiSwap v3 FAQ
What is SushiSwap v3?

A decentralized exchange protocol from Sushi where smart-contract pools swap token pairs without an order book or custodian, and liquidity providers concentrate capital in chosen price ranges to earn pool fees. It follows the Uniswap v3-style architecture on Sushi's own deployments across multiple EVM networks.

Is this the official SushiSwap site?

No. This is an independent reference dashboard about SushiSwap v3 — it does not custody assets or execute trades. Swaps and positions happen in your own wallet against the live contracts, and you should always confirm you are using the genuine Sushi interface before connecting.

Does a swap need a token approval?

Yes, the first time you spend a given ERC-20 through the router: you sign an approval transaction granting the contract an allowance over that token, then sign the swap separately. The approval is a standing permission, not the trade itself, so check the spender address and amount before signing.

What does a swap on SushiSwap v3 cost?

Three components: the pool's fee tier deducted from the output, network gas paid to the chain, and price impact from your trade size against available liquidity. The fee structure is set per pool, so read the quoted route rather than assuming a fixed number.

Which networks does SushiSwap v3 support?

Ethereum mainnet (chain ID 1) plus numerous other published EVM deployments, each with its own pools and liquidity. Availability and depth of a given pair vary per chain — check the live app on the exact network, and the SushiSwap v3 tracker on DefiLlama for per-chain activity.

Is SushiSwap v3 safe to use?

It is non-custodial and the contracts are open-source, but smart-contract bugs, malicious tokens, phishing interfaces, approval misuse and adverse price moves remain real risks. Verify the interface, the token contracts and every signature, and track the SUSHI token separately from protocol safety — one says nothing about the other.

Notes before you swap on SushiSwap v3

Practitioner's note: concentrated liquidity rewards attention — an in-range position earns, an out-of-range one sits idle, and a swap is only as good as the route you actually read before signing.

The common mistake is approving and swapping on autopilot: clicking through the approval, ignoring the price impact line, and discovering afterwards that the route filled through a thin pool at a worse price than the headline quote suggested.

Before you sign:

  • Confirm the wallet is on the right network for the tokens you hold.
  • Read the price impact and minimum received on the quote — if impact is high, the route is thin.
  • Check the approval's spender address and allowance amount, and verify the token by contract address, not by ticker.
